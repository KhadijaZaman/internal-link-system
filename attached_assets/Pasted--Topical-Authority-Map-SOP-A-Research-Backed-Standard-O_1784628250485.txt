## A Research-Backed Standard Operating Procedure for Building Topical Authority on Any Website

**Version 1.0 · July 2026**

# 1. What Topical Authority Is (and How Google Likely Understands It)

## 1.1 Definition

**Topical authority is a ranking state, not a content style.** A site has topical authority when search engines treat it as a trusted, comprehensive source for a subject area, and rank its pages for queries in that area with less dependence on page-level link signals.

The most precise working definition comes from Koray Tuğberk GÜBÜR **[FRAMEWORK]**:

> Topical Authority = **Topical Coverage × Historical Data**

- **Topical Coverage** = complete, structured, *connected* information processing. If you didn't define X, you didn't cover it. If you didn't connect X to Y, you didn't cover it. More pages ≠ more coverage.
- **Historical Data** = the quality of user engagement with your content over time — not domain age. This signal lags roughly six months: today's rankings reflect actions taken at least half a year ago.

## 1.2 Why it matters

1. **[FACT]** Google evaluates site-level signals, not just page-level ones. Google's documentation on its helpful content signals states that sitewide classifiers assess whether a site produces people-first content, and the Quality Rater Guidelines instruct raters to assess E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) partly at the site and author level.
2. **[FACT]** Google representatives have publicly stated that Google understands "authority of content and the notion of topics" — a site with a history of publishing authoritative content on a topic can be seen as an authority in that area.
3. **[BEST PRACTICE]** Topical authority reduces per-page link dependence. Ahrefs, Semrush, and Moz all document cases where lower-DR sites outrank higher-DR sites for niche queries because their topic-level coverage is deeper. Semrush surveys report that the overwhelming majority of SEOs treat topical authority as a core part of content strategy.
4. **AI search amplifies it.** Generative engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews/AI Mode) retrieve and cite sources at the passage and entity level. Sites with dense, consistent, well-connected coverage of an entity get retrieved more often. **[BEST PRACTICE — increasingly documented by AI-visibility research, still an evolving area]**

## 1.3 How Google likely understands topical authority

No single "topical authority score" is documented. The evidence converges from several directions:

| Mechanism | Evidence tier | What it suggests |
|---|---|---|
| **Semantic/entity-based search** (Hummingbird 2013 →, Knowledge Graph, BERT, MUM) | **[FACT]** these systems exist | Google resolves queries and documents to *entities and relationships*, not just keywords. Covering an entity's attributes comprehensively maps directly to how the index is organized. |
| **Topical TrustRank** (research paper cited in Google's updated PageRank patent) | **[PATENT]** | The web is partitioned into topical "shards"; trusted seed sites are chosen *per topic*, so even a small niche can contain an authority site. Link distance from topic-trusted seeds matters more than raw link volume. |
| **Categorical quality scores** (patents analyzed by Bill Slawski) | **[PATENT]** | Google may compute quality scores for site sections/categories of content — i.e., quality is assessed at topic level, not only page or domain level. |
| **Phrase-based indexing** (Slawski's patent analyses) | **[PATENT]** | Documents are scored partly on the presence of *related phrases* that co-occur in expert documents on a topic — a direct algorithmic analogue of "covering the topic the way experts do." |
| **Reasonable Surfer model** | **[PATENT]** | Link value flows with click probability and context — contextual internal links in main content carry more weight than boilerplate links. |
| **Helpful content / site-level classifiers** | **[FACT]** | Sitewide signals can suppress or lift entire sites, confirming that Google models sites, not just pages. |
| **E-E-A-T in the Quality Rater Guidelines** | **[FACT]** (as an evaluation rubric, not a direct ranking factor) | Raters assess whether the *site and author* are recognized sources for the topic. QRG data trains and validates ranking systems. |

**Synthesis [BEST PRACTICE]:** Google most plausibly models topical authority as the intersection of (a) entity-level coverage breadth and depth, (b) consistency and factual alignment across a site's documents, (c) engagement/historical signals for the topic, and (d) topic-relevant link/citation proximity. A topical authority map is the planning artifact that maximizes (a) and (b) deliberately, so that (c) and (d) can accumulate.

## 1.4 Entities, semantics, context, and coverage — how they relate

- An **entity** is a distinct thing (person, product, concept) with **attributes** (properties) and **relationships** to other entities. Search engines resolve queries to entities and attributes ("mobile phone" + "battery life").
- **Semantics** is how meaning is constructed — lexical relations (synonyms, hypernyms), query semantics (search language ≠ natural language), and frame semantics (predicates like *buy, compare, fix* determine context).
- **Context** is the specific angle from which an entity is processed. The same entity produces entirely different topical maps for different **source contexts**: "glasses" for an eye doctor vs. a fashion stylist vs. a manufacturer **[FRAMEWORK — Koray]**. This is the single most-ignored principle in topical mapping.
- **Topical coverage** is the degree to which your site processes the entity's attributes, related entities, and query networks *from your source context* — completely, consistently, and with everything connected.

**The causal chain:** Source context → central entity → attributes → query networks → pages → internal links → coverage → (over time, with engagement) → authority.

# 2. Planning Framework

Before any research, five planning decisions must be made in order. Skipping these produces the generic keyword-cluster spreadsheets that fail.

## 2.1 Define the source context (who you are, how you make money)

**[FRAMEWORK — Koray; converges with Google's "people-first content" self-assessment questions, which ask whether content serves your site's primary purpose and existing audience [FACT]]**

Answer in one paragraph:
- What does the business sell, and to whom?
- What is the monetization model (SaaS subscription, affiliate, e-commerce, leads, ads)?
- What is the logical bridge between the content and the money?

*Everything downstream depends on this.* A topic that is "high volume" but disconnected from the monetization bridge belongs in the outer section or nowhere.

## 2.2 Identify the primary topical entity (central entity)

The central entity is the single entity that will appear across every piece of content in the network. Tests for choosing it correctly:

- It must be specific enough to own ("AI visibility," not "marketing").
- It must be broad enough to support a full query network (hundreds of entity-attribute pairs).
- Every product/service you sell must be expressible as an attribute, sub-entity, or outcome of it.
- List its synonyms and alternate names now — they belong in the map.

## 2.3 Determine topical relevance (what counts as "on-topic")

A candidate topic is relevant if it passes the three-filter test, applied **in this order** **[FRAMEWORK — Koray's attribute filtering]**:

1. **Relevant** — to the central entity AND your source context (how you make money).
2. **Prominent** — appears in titles, People Also Ask, and featured snippets of top-ranking content. Litmus test: *can the entity be defined without this attribute?* (Population is prominent for a country; a football league is not.)
3. **Popular** — has sufficient search demand (checked last, never first).

Most teams filter by popularity first. That is backwards: it optimizes for volume, not authority.

## 2.4 Define content scope via topical borders

**[FRAMEWORK — Koray]** Write two explicit lists: **WILL cover** and **WILL NOT cover**. Borders are determined by user behaviors, entities in queries, and query context — not by what competitors happen to publish. Two consequences:

- When competitors expand their borders, your *relative* coverage shrinks even if your content is unchanged. Monitor and respond by **deepening** inside your borders, not by widening into their space.
- Generic "101" content that every site in the niche has already covered usually dilutes rather than builds authority for a specialized site. **[BEST PRACTICE — supported by Google's "information gain" patent [PATENT] and by helpful-content guidance discouraging content made to seem comprehensive without adding value [FACT]]**

## 2.5 Create the topical hierarchy

Structure the map in two macro-sections plus a spine:

| Layer | Role | Predicates | Monetization |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core Section** | The central entity's main attributes + central search intent + source context | know, use, compare, buy | Direct — drives revenue |
| **Outer Section** | Connected query networks outside the core | know | Indirect — builds historical data and topical relevance |
| **Central Search Intent** | The one sentence unifying the whole map (e.g., "find and compare the best X") | — | The spine connecting both |

Within each section: **Pillar → Core topics → Supporting topics → Subtopics** (detailed in §5).

# 3. Research Process (Repeatable, With Tools)

Run these eight research workstreams in order. Each produces a concrete artifact that feeds the SOP in §4.

## 3.1 Competitor analysis (authority source reverse-engineering)

**Goal:** Identify the sites Google already classifies as authorities for your topic, and understand *why* — so your site can be classified alongside them during Broad Core Algorithm Updates. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray; converges with Topical TrustRank's per-topic seed logic [PATENT]]**

**Process:**
1. Identify the 3–5 sites with the most topical coverage + organic traffic in your borders (not the biggest domains — the deepest *topical* ones).
2. Pick ONE main authority source to reverse-engineer.
3. Export their sitemap URLs, page titles, H1–H6, and internal anchor texts (Screaming Frog or any SEO crawler).
4. Export their full organic keyword profile and top pages with top keyword + volume (Ahrefs Site Explorer / Semrush Organic Research).
5. Extract n-grams from titles/headings to reveal their query templates and attribute patterns.

**Tools:** Ahrefs Site Explorer + Batch Analysis, Semrush Organic Research, Screaming Frog, Sitebulb.

## 3.2 SERP analysis

**Goal:** Extract what Google itself reveals about the query space.

Extract, per seed query: autocomplete suggestions, related searches, People Also Ask, search refinement tabs (including image search tabs), knowledge panels, "Things to know," ranking page titles and meta descriptions, bolded terms in snippets, and — for AI-era coverage — AI Overview presence and cited sources.

**Tools:** Manual SERP review, Ahrefs SERP Overview, Semrush SERP Features report, AlsoAsked (PAA trees), Thruuu or Screaming Frog SERP scraping, DataForSEO SERP API for scale.

## 3.3 Entity extraction

**Goal:** Build the entity inventory — the central entity's attributes, sub-entities, and related entities.

**Process:**
1. Analyze the Wikipedia article for the central entity: table of contents, internal links, categories, references. Cross-check Wikidata properties. **[BEST PRACTICE — Wikipedia's structure approximates consensus entity-attribute organization]**
2. Prompt LLMs: *"List all the entities/nouns/relationships related to [entity]"* and *"List all the lexical relations of [entity]."* Treat outputs as candidates, never facts — validate against SERPs.
3. Run competitor top pages through an entity-recognition tool.
4. Classify each attribute as **root** (appears in nearly all instances of the entity type), **rare** (some instances), or **unique** (one instance). Priority for relevance: **unique > root > rare** **[FRAMEWORK — Koray]**.

**Tools:** Wikipedia/Wikidata, Google Cloud Natural Language API, InLinks, WordLift, TextRazor, any frontier LLM (with validation).

## 3.4 Keyword clustering

**Goal:** Group the compiled query data so that one page = one canonical query cluster.

**Process:**
1. Compile all queries from GSC (filter: query contains entity; page contains entity), SERP extraction, competitor exports, and keyword tools (Ahrefs Matching Terms, Semrush Broad Match).
2. Separate **query data** from **query templates** ("best [X] under [price]", "how to fix [issue] on [device]") — templates generate coverage systematically across entity instances.
3. Cluster by **SERP overlap** (queries whose top-10 results share 3+ URLs belong on one page) supplemented by semantic similarity. SERP-based clustering is the ground truth; embedding-based clustering is the scale assist. **[BEST PRACTICE]**
4. Deduplicate. Assign each cluster a single **canonical query** — the query the page will "own."

**Tools:** Keyword Insights, Cluster AI, Semrush Keyword Manager, Ahrefs (parent topic), custom embeddings + SERP-overlap scripts.

## 3.5 Search intent mapping

**Goal:** Tag every cluster with intent and funnel stage.

- Intent types: informational / commercial investigation / transactional / navigational.
- Express intent through **predicates** (know, learn, compare, buy, fix, go) — predicates determine context more precisely than the four-way taxonomy. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray's frame semantics]**
- Verify against the live SERP: the ranking page *types* (guides vs. category pages vs. product pages) are Google's declaration of the dominant intent. **[BEST PRACTICE]**
- Map clusters to journey stages (see §5.9).

**Tools:** Manual SERP inspection (non-negotiable), Semrush intent labels (assist only), Keyword Insights intent classification.

## 3.6 Topic gap analysis

**Goal:** Find what authorities cover that you don't — and what nobody covers well.

1. Content gap: your domain vs. 3–5 authority sources (Ahrefs Content Gap / Semrush Keyword Gap).
2. Coverage gap: attributes present in your entity inventory but absent from *everyone's* content → **information gain opportunities** (see §6.7).
3. Question gap: PAA and forum questions (Reddit, Quora, niche communities) with no dedicated answer in the SERP.

**Tools:** Ahrefs Content Gap, Semrush Keyword Gap, AlsoAsked, manual forum mining, GSC (queries with impressions but no dedicated page).

## 3.7 Semantic relationship analysis

**Goal:** Decide how topics connect — which pages are parents, siblings, and bridges.

1. Classify topics by **local proximity** (how users group them behaviorally), not dictionary taxonomy. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray]**
2. Identify **contextual bridges**: the sub-topic where two pages logically hand off to each other (this becomes the internal link placement).
3. Verify each planned connection has a real semantic justification. *A contextless link is a worthless link.*

**Tools:** Your entity map + clustering sheet; InLinks knowledge graph view; manual editorial judgment.

## 3.8 Internal linking opportunity analysis

**Goal:** Plan the link graph before writing.

1. From the hierarchy: every child links up to its parent; parents link down to children; siblings link where a contextual bridge exists.
2. Anchor text rule: anchors must match (or be lemmatized versions of) the target page's title/H1. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray; converges with Google's documented recommendation to use descriptive anchor text [FACT]]**
3. Link placement: after the linked concept has been defined and its related question answered — never on the first words of a page. **[FRAMEWORK]**
4. For existing sites: crawl to find orphan pages, over-linked boilerplate, and pages whose strongest semantic sibling isn't linked.

**Tools:** Screaming Frog (crawl + link graph), Sitebulb, Ahrefs Internal Link Opportunities report, InLinks.

### Research workstream → tool summary

| Workstream | Primary tools | Output artifact |
|---|---|---|
| Competitor analysis | Ahrefs, Semrush, Screaming Frog | Authority source coverage export |
| SERP analysis | Manual + AlsoAsked + SERP APIs | SERP extraction sheet |
| Entity extraction | Wikipedia/Wikidata, NLP APIs, LLMs | Entity map (Template B) |
| Keyword clustering | Keyword Insights, SERP overlap | Keyword cluster sheet (Template C) |
| Intent mapping | Manual SERP inspection | Intent + funnel tags on clusters |
| Gap analysis | Ahrefs/Semrush gap tools, forums | Gap list with information-gain flags |
| Semantic relationships | Entity map + judgment | Bridge/connection list |
| Internal linking | Screaming Frog, Sitebulb | Internal linking plan (Template F) |

# 4. The SOP — Step by Step

Ten steps, start to finish. Time estimates assume one experienced operator; a junior SEO should budget 1.5–2×.

---

## Step 1 — Pin the Source Context & Central Entity

- **Objective:** Lock the foundation every later decision inherits.
- **Inputs:** Business model docs, product/pricing pages, sales conversations, existing audience data.
- **Actions:**
  1. Write the one-paragraph source context (who, what's sold, to whom, monetization bridge).
  2. Choose the central entity; list all synonyms and alternate names.
  3. Write the central search intent as one sentence with predicates (e.g., "[Audience] wants to **know** how X works, **compare** options, and **buy** Y").
  4. Draft topical borders v0 (WILL / WILL NOT cover).
- **Expected output:** A one-page Source Context Charter, signed off by whoever owns revenue.
- **Quality checks:** Can every product be expressed as an attribute/outcome of the central entity? Would a different business plausibly write the same charter? (If yes, it's too generic.)
- **Common mistakes:** Choosing an entity so broad it can't be owned; letting "SEO-attractive" topics define the entity instead of the business.
- **Best practices:** Get commercial stakeholders to veto the borders now — it prevents the "why are we writing about this?" conversation six months in.

## Step 2 — Model the Ontology, Personas & Journey

- **Objective:** Understand the entity's structure and the humans searching for it before touching keyword tools.
- **Inputs:** Source Context Charter; customer research; sales/support transcripts.
- **Actions:**
  1. Build the ontology: entity types, attributes, relationships, hierarchies (LLM-assisted, human-validated).
  2. Define 2–4 personas in priority order, each with jobs-to-be-done and the questions they ask at each journey stage.
  3. Map the buyer's journey stages you will serve (see §5.9). Content chosen for "topical authority" without a journey mapping produces coverage that never converts — a failure mode called out by practitioners analyzing Google's people-first guidance. **[BEST PRACTICE]**
- **Expected output:** Ontology diagram + persona/journey one-pager.
- **Quality checks:** Every persona maps to at least one monetized (core section) topic area.
- **Common mistakes:** Skipping personas because "we know our audience"; ontologies copied from Wikipedia without source-context filtering.
- **Best practices:** Interview sales/support — the vocabulary customers actually use becomes query-template input.

## Step 3 — Identify & Reverse-Engineer Authority Sources

- **Objective:** Learn how the sites Google already trusts structure the topic.
- **Inputs:** Borders v0; access to Ahrefs/Semrush + a crawler.
- **Actions:** Run §3.1 in full. Produce, for the main authority source: full URL inventory, title/heading n-grams, organic query export, top pages with volumes.
- **Expected output:** Authority Source Analysis workbook.
- **Quality checks:** The chosen authority is topical (deep in your borders), not just big. Their coverage patterns are documented as reusable templates, not just admired.
- **Common mistakes:** Reverse-engineering a general-news domain because its DR is high; copying their site structure wholesale (their source context differs from yours).
- **Best practices:** Note what they *don't* cover — that's your information-gain shortlist.

## Step 4 — Gather All Query & Entity Data

- **Objective:** Compile the raw material — every query, entity, and attribute in scope.
- **Inputs:** Outputs of Steps 1–3; GSC access (for existing sites).
- **Actions:** Run §3.2–3.3 and compile into one Initial Data Sheet with columns: Query | Volume | Competing URLs | Technique (authority hacking / lexical semantics / historical data / query semantics) | Source (GSC, Ahrefs, SERP, LLM, competitor X).
- **Expected output:** Initial Data Sheet (typically 2,000–20,000 rows depending on niche).
- **Quality checks:** ≥4 independent data sources represented; GSC data included for existing sites; every LLM-sourced entity validated against at least one SERP.
- **Common mistakes:** Single-tool dependence (each keyword database has blind spots); treating LLM output as ground truth.
- **Best practices:** Keep provenance columns — six months later you'll want to know where a topic came from.

## Step 5 — Cluster, Filter & Prioritize

- **Objective:** Turn raw data into a deduplicated topic list where one topic = one page = one canonical query.
- **Inputs:** Initial Data Sheet.
- **Actions:**
  1. Cluster by SERP overlap + semantic similarity (§3.4).
  2. Apply the three filters **in order**: Relevant → Prominent → Popular. Drop what fails.
  3. Assign priority: core feature + foundational → HIGH; core function + high interest → HIGH; emerging trend aligned with brand → MEDIUM; popular but unrelated to brand → LOW; legacy tactic off-positioning → LOW.
  4. Sequence for momentum: challenging important topics first, not easy wins first. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray; DEBATED — many practitioners sequence quick wins first for stakeholder buy-in; choose based on runway]**
- **Expected output:** Filtered, prioritized topic list with canonical queries.
- **Quality checks:** No two topics share a canonical query; every HIGH topic traces to source context; cluster count is defensible (spot-check 10 clusters against live SERPs).
- **Common mistakes:** Filtering by volume first; splitting one intent across multiple thin pages (cannibalization by design).
- **Best practices:** Write a one-line border rule per cluster ("this page covers X; defers Y to [other page]") — it becomes the brief's topical border section.

## Step 6 — Build the Topical Authority Map

- **Objective:** Assemble the full map: hierarchy, sections, metadata, connections.
- **Inputs:** Prioritized topic list; ontology; borders.
- **Actions:**
  1. Assign every topic to Core or Outer section.
  2. Arrange the hierarchy: Pillar → Core topics → Supporting topics → Subtopics (§5).
  3. Add macro-semantic metadata per node: title tag, URL slug, meta description, page type, content format, funnel stage, attribute owned.
  4. Plan internal link targets and anchor text per node (anchors = target's title/H1, lemmatized).
  5. Write final topical borders (WILL / WILL NOT) at map level.
- **Expected output:** The Topical Authority Map (Template A) — the master planning artifact.
- **Quality checks:** Every node has exactly one canonical query and one attribute; URL structure mirrors hierarchy (root → seed → node, 1–2 words per segment, no stop words unless meaningful); no orphan nodes in the planned link graph.
- **Common mistakes:** Nodes without owned attributes ("10 tips" pages); URL slugs that don't reflect hierarchy; forgetting the outer section entirely (all-commercial maps stall on historical data).
- **Best practices:** Deliberately repeat key words across core-section titles — aggregated title vocabulary defines site topicality. **[FRAMEWORK]**

## Step 7 — Write Content Briefs

- **Objective:** Turn each map node into an unambiguous writing instruction.
- **Inputs:** Map node + SERP/PAA data for its canonical query.
- **Actions:** For each node produce (Template E): main content spec (central entity, macro context, attributes, contextual bridges), supplementary content spec (minor entities, micro contexts, question variations), the contextual vector (ordered headings with levels), per-section methodology (format, modality, evidence requirements), and the anchor/link plan.
- **Expected output:** One brief per node, batched by cluster.
- **Quality checks:** Heading vector is "straight" (every heading has a point and follows from the last); every declaration is followed by an implied question the next section answers; the brief specifies where each internal link is placed (after the concept is defined, never first words).
- **Common mistakes:** Briefs that are just heading lists with word counts; supplementary content written as exhaustively as main content (it should be brief answers + links).
- **Best practices:** Answer-first structure: the first sentence under any question heading answers it directly, reusing the question's words — this is both a featured-snippet tactic **[BEST PRACTICE]** and an inquisitive-semantics rule **[FRAMEWORK]**.

## Step 8 — Produce & Publish With Momentum

- **Objective:** Ship the map with consistent cadence and consistent facts.
- **Inputs:** Briefs; writers/editors; style + fact glossary.
- **Actions:**
  1. Publish in cluster batches (a supporting cluster with its parent beats scattered singles).
  2. Maintain publication frequency — search engines need consistent signals; vary exact days/times within a steady cadence. **[FRAMEWORK; DEBATED — cadence-as-signal is contested, but consistency's indirect benefits (crawl demand, freshness, compounding coverage) are broadly accepted [BEST PRACTICE]]**
  3. Enforce factual consistency: numbers and definitions must match across every page. Inconsistency makes the network look random to search engines. **[FRAMEWORK]**
  4. Implement all internal links from the plan at publish time, not "later."
- **Expected output:** Published clusters with live link graph and valid schema.
- **Quality checks:** Run the 16-point quality audit (§9.7 / Appendix in checklist) on every core-section page before publishing; spot-audit outer section.
- **Common mistakes:** Publishing pillars months before their clusters (thin hub syndrome); links deferred and forgotten; new pages while the site's ranking state is negative — if rankings have collapsed, audit/consolidate/fix links FIRST, then expand. **[FRAMEWORK — Koray's negative-ranking-state rule]**
- **Best practices:** Keep quality nodes (the most important articles) close to the homepage in click depth — they carry the authority signals your link graph distributes.

## Step 9 — Evaluate the Authority State

- **Objective:** Measure whether the ranking state is shifting (§12 metrics).
- **Inputs:** GSC, analytics, rank tracking, crawler.
- **Actions:** At 30/90/180 days: impressions growth by topic cluster (impressions lead clicks — they signal Google testing your coverage); new/lost/shared queries per page; click-depth and orphan checks; coverage % vs. map.
- **Expected output:** Quarterly Authority Evaluation report.
- **Quality checks:** Analysis is per-cluster, not sitewide averages (averages hide which sections are working).
- **Common mistakes:** Judging at 6 weeks (historical data lags ~6 months); reacting to individual keyword wobbles instead of cluster trends.
- **Best practices:** Track *impressions first, clicks second* in the first two quarters — that ordering matches how trust accrues. **[FRAMEWORK]**

## Step 10 — Refine, Reconfigure, Expand

- **Objective:** Treat the map as living — never freeze it.
- **Inputs:** Evaluation report; competitor publication monitoring.
- **Actions:** Update pages (add info, remove context-diluting sections, adjust links); consolidate thin/overlapping pages; expand borders only when the current section's coverage is complete; log every map version with date and rationale.
- **Expected output:** Map v2, v3… with changelog.
- **Quality checks:** Decision rule for each new topic — new page if it's a new core/outer node; expand existing page if it's a deeper attribute of that page's entity; supplementary content if a full page would dilute macro context.
- **Common mistakes:** Endless net-new publishing while existing pages decay; widening borders reactively every time a competitor publishes.
- **Best practices:** Consolidate before expanding ("lean management"). Re-run the gap analysis quarterly.

# 5. The Topical Authority Map Framework

## 5.1 The layers

| Layer | Definition | Typical count | Example (personal finance) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pillar topic** | The central entity itself, or a top-level attribute set; owns the broadest canonical query | 1–5 | "Budgeting" |
| **Core topic** | A main attribute of the central entity, tied to central search intent | 5–15 per pillar | "Zero-based budgeting" |
| **Supporting topic** | A facet, comparison, use case, or process of a core topic | 3–10 per core topic | "Zero-based vs. 50/30/20" |
| **Subtopic** | A specific question, variation, or long-tail node | as needed | "Zero-based budgeting with irregular income" |

## 5.2 Entities & attributes

Every node in the map owns **one entity-attribute pair**. That pair *is* the page's macro context — one focus per page. Multiple parallel topics on one page = diluted macro context = a page that ranks for nothing decisively.

## 5.3 Questions

Generate questions systematically from attributes using interrogatives (what/who/when/where/why/how) crossed with attribute types (root/rare/unique). The **representative question** gets the fullest answer first; **represented variations** (demographic, situational) follow in descending probability order.

## 5.4 Search intents & predicates

Tag every node: intent type + predicate + the exact canonical query it owns. Core section biases toward compare/use/buy predicates; outer section is almost entirely *know*.

## 5.5 User journey stages

| Stage | Funnel | Typical node types | Section |
|---|---|---|---|
| Problem-unaware / learning | TOFU | Definitions, concepts, how-X-works | Outer |
| Solution-aware / evaluating | MOFU | Comparisons, methods, best-of, use cases | Core |
| Decision / acting | BOFU | Product-adjacent guides, alternatives, pricing context | Core |
| Post-purchase / mastery | Retention | How-tos, troubleshooting, advanced tactics | Outer/Core |

## 5.6 How everything connects

```mermaid
graph TD
    SC[Source Context<br/>who you are + how you monetize] --> CE[Central Entity]
    CE --> CSI[Central Search Intent]
    CSI --> CORE[CORE SECTION<br/>main attributes · compare/buy predicates]
    CSI --> OUTER[OUTER SECTION<br/>connected query networks · know predicate]
    CORE --> P1[Pillar page]
    P1 --> C1[Core topic A]
    P1 --> C2[Core topic B]
    C1 --> S1[Supporting topic A1]
    C1 --> S2[Supporting topic A2]
    S1 --> Q1[Subtopic / question node]
    OUTER --> O1[Outer cluster 1]
    OUTER --> O2[Outer cluster 2]
    O1 -.contextual bridge.-> C1
    O2 -.contextual bridge.-> C2
    C2 -.sibling bridge.-> C1
```

Solid lines = parent-child (hierarchy + URL structure + primary internal links). Dotted lines = contextual bridges (cross-links justified by a shared sub-topic). Outer-section clusters always bridge back to a core node — that bridge is how historical data earned on informational content transfers relevance to money pages.

## 5.7 Internal linking structure rules

1. Child → parent: always. Parent → children: always (defined list, most important first).
2. Sibling ↔ sibling: only across a real contextual bridge.
3. Anchor text = target page's title/H1 (or lemmatized form). Consistency here builds the knowledge-base effect.
4. Place links after the linked concept is defined and its local question answered.
5. 2–4 contextual links per 1,000 words as a working density norm **[BEST PRACTICE]**; boilerplate/sidebar links carry less weight than in-content links (Reasonable Surfer **[PATENT]**).
6. Quality nodes sit within 1–2 clicks of the homepage.
7. Strategic asymmetry: link *to* your most important pages; a strong page need not always link back. **[FRAMEWORK]**

# 6. Content Architecture

## 6.1 Topic clusters
A cluster = one parent page + its supporting pages + the dense link mesh among them. The cluster, not the page, is the unit of coverage. Popularized by HubSpot's pillar-cluster model **[FRAMEWORK — HubSpot]**; algorithmically motivated by phrase co-occurrence and site-level classification **[PATENT/FACT]**.

## 6.2 Content hubs vs. silos
- **Hub** = everything connected to a root (ontology view). **Silo** = theme-based branches (taxonomy view). Use both, in layers: root linked to homepage → sections expand outward → interlinking within sections → everything paths back to root. This creates a contextual crawl path, mirrored by URL structure (`/topic/subtopic/detail`).
- **[DEBATED]** Strict silos (no cross-silo links ever) are outdated; cross-links across real contextual bridges outperform hermetic silos.

## 6.3 Parent-child relationships
Parent defines the concept and links down with a defined list; children go deep on one attribute and link up. The parent's heading vector previews children's canonical queries — that's what makes the hub feel complete without being bloated.

## 6.4 Semantic bridges
A bridge is a shared sub-concept that lets two pages connect without diluting either macro context (e.g., "budgeting apps" bridges "budgeting methods" ↔ "personal finance software"). Bridges are found in §3.7 and written as short transition segments in supplementary content.

## 6.5 Contextual links
A link's value = relevance of the anchor + surrounding passage to the target. Google documents descriptive anchors **[FACT]**; the Reasonable Surfer model implies contextual main-content links outweigh navigational ones **[PATENT]**. Practical rule: if you can't write a sentence that naturally defines the target before linking, the link doesn't belong there.

## 6.6 Topical depth
Depth = covering an attribute past the surface: definitions → instances → numbers → edge cases → conflicting views → next actions. Depth is measured against the entity inventory, not word count. Two 1,200-word pages covering distinct attributes beat one 4,000-word page mixing them.

## 6.7 Information gain
**[PATENT + BEST PRACTICE]** Google's "contextual estimation of link information gain" patent describes scoring documents by *new information relative to what the user has already seen*. Practical translation: every page must contain something the current top 10 doesn't — original data, first-hand testing, a named expert's input, a sharper framework, or attribute coverage the SERP lacks. Pages that only recombine the existing SERP add zero gain and dilute the site's quality profile.

## 6.8 Content uniqueness
Target ≥90% uniqueness and <20% phrase overlap with competitors. Uniqueness includes *structural* uniqueness (your own ordering and framing) and *evidentiary* uniqueness (your data, your examples, your images). Unique, context-relevant images are themselves a quality signal. **[FRAMEWORK; converges with helpful-content guidance [FACT]]**

# 7. Semantic SEO Integration

How Koray's semantic framework **[FRAMEWORK throughout this section]** plugs into the SOP:

| Principle | What it means | Where it lives in this SOP |
|---|---|---|
| **Context vectors** | The ordered heading sequence from first word to last; must be "straight" — every step relevant, every step with a point | Step 7 briefs; brief template |
| **Topical maps** | The planning artifact connecting source context → entity → sections → nodes | Step 6; Template A |
| **Topical coverage** | Complete + structured + connected processing of the entity | The whole SOP; measured in §12 |
| **Entity relationships** | EAV (entity-attribute-value) triples with consistent predicates across the site | Step 4 entity extraction; Template B |
| **Lexical semantics** | Synonyms, hypernyms/hyponyms, antonyms of the entity — coverage includes its lexical field | §3.3; include synonyms in titles/headings deliberately |
| **Query semantics** | Search language ≠ natural language; meaning bends inside queries; query templates generate coverage systematically | Steps 4–5 |
| **Search intent evolution** | Intents shift over time (and now, per-engine: classic SERP vs. AI answers); re-verify SERPs quarterly | Step 9–10 |
| **Information gain** | Add what the SERP lacks | §6.7; brief quality check |
| **Content completeness** | Every declaration raises a question the content then answers (inquisitive semantics); all interpretations of ambiguous queries covered | Step 7; QA checklist |
| **Topical expansion** | Widen borders only after current coverage is complete; expansion follows consolidation | Step 10 |

Four authorship rules worth enforcing in every brief because they're cheap and high-leverage:
1. **Answer first, define later** — never delay the answer.
2. **Be definite near contextual borders** — "is/are" for facts; reserve "can/may/might" for research-based possibilities and "should" for recommendations, and use them accurately (modality is information).
3. **Numbers everywhere** — content without numeric values doesn't read as expertise (be precise: 3.785, not "about 3.7").
4. **Examples after plurals** — "over 400 cryptocurrencies, such as Ethereum, Bitcoin, and Litecoin."

# 9. Checklists

## 9.1 Pre-research checklist
- [ ] Source context written and approved by revenue owner
- [ ] Central entity chosen; synonyms listed

- [ ] Topical borders v0 drafted (WILL / WILL NOT)

- [ ] For existing sites: ranking state assessed (positive / building / negative) — if negative, plan an audit-first sequence

## 9.2 Research checklist
- [ ] 3–5 authority sources identified; ONE selected for reverse-engineering
- [ ] Authority source: sitemap, titles, headings, anchors, organic queries exported
- [ ] GSC exports pulled (query contains entity; page contains entity)
- [ ] SERP extraction done per seed query (autocomplete, related, PAA, tabs, knowledge panel, AI Overview citations)
- [ ] Wikipedia/Wikidata analyzed for the central entity
- [ ] LLM entity/lexical-relation prompts run AND validated against SERPs
- [ ] Query templates separated from query data
- [ ] Initial Data Sheet compiled with provenance columns

## 9.3 Mapping checklist
- [ ] Clusters built on SERP overlap (spot-checked ×10)
- [ ] Filters applied in order: Relevant → Prominent → Popular
- [ ] One canonical query per node; zero duplicates
- [ ] Every node assigned: Core/Outer, attribute, page type, format, funnel stage, priority
- [ ] Title tags, URL slugs, meta descriptions drafted per node
- [ ] URL hierarchy mirrors topical hierarchy (root → seed → node)
- [ ] Internal link targets + anchors planned per node
- [ ] Map-level borders finalized and written into the map

## 9.4 Content planning checklist
- [ ] Brief exists for every node in the current batch
- [ ] Each brief: main vs. supplementary content separated explicitly
- [ ] Contextual vector (heading sequence) is straight — no drift
- [ ] Answer-first rule specified for every question heading
- [ ] Evidence requirements listed (named sources, numbers, examples)
- [ ] Anchor text plan matches target titles/H1s
- [ ] Information-gain element identified per page (what the SERP lacks)

## 9.5 Publishing checklist
- [ ] Cluster batch published together (parent + supporting)
- [ ] All planned internal links live at publish
- [ ] Schema valid on every page
- [ ] Facts/numbers checked against the site glossary (consistency)
- [ ] Unique images with macro-context-relevant ALTs
- [ ] Author bio + credentials present
- [ ] Publication cadence maintained per plan

## 9.6 Internal linking checklist
- [ ] No orphan pages (crawl-verified)
- [ ] Child→parent and parent→children links present
- [ ] Sibling links only across real contextual bridges
- [ ] Anchors match target title/H1 (lemmatized OK)
- [ ] Links placed after concept definition, never on opening words
- [ ] Quality nodes ≤2 clicks from homepage
- [ ] 2–4 in-content links per 1,000 words; ≥95% topically relevant

## 9.7 QA checklist (pre-publish, per page — condensed from the 16-point audit)
- [ ] Factuality ≥85%; claims dated/sourced
- [ ] Zero contradictions with other site pages
- [ ] Topic drift <10%; heading vector straight
- [ ] Q&A structure where the query network demands it; answers reuse question words
- [ ] 15–20 unique entities per 1,000 words, mostly with attributes
- [ ] Sentences avg 15–20 words; ≥80% active voice; grade 8–10 readability
- [ ] <1% filler; no redundant phrasing
- [ ] ≥90% unique vs. competitors (<20% phrase overlap)
- [ ] First words of H1/H2s/paragraphs lead with the entity or intent phrase
- [ ] Definite modality at contextual borders; hedging only where accurate

# 11. Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Each)

| # | Mistake | Why it fails | Avoidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Volume-first topic selection** | Optimizes for demand, not authority; produces off-context content | Filter Relevant → Prominent → Popular, in that order |
| 2 | **No source context** | Same map as everyone else; content the business can't monetize | Step 1 charter before any research |
| 3 | **Page count as coverage** | More pages ≠ coverage; thin fragmentation triggers cannibalization and quality suppression | One canonical query per page; consolidate overlaps |
| 4 | **All-core maps (no outer section)** | No historical data engine; commercial pages stall without informational relevance transfer | Plan the outer section and its bridges deliberately |
| 5 | **Copying a competitor's map wholesale** | Their source context ≠ yours; you inherit their irrelevancies and miss your angles | Reverse-engineer patterns, then re-filter through YOUR context |
| 6 | **Ignoring topical borders** | Scope creep dilutes topicality; "AI for everything" sites rank for nothing | Written WILL/WILL-NOT lists; deepen before widening |
| 7 | **Briefs without contextual structure** | Writers produce heading-stuffed articles with topic drift | Contextual vector + answer-first + modality rules in every brief |
| 8 | **Inconsistent facts across pages** | The network reads as random; knowledge-base effect never forms | Site fact glossary; consistency check at publish |
| 9 | **Internal links as an afterthought** | Coverage exists but isn't *connected* — half the definition of coverage unmet | Link plan in the map; links live at publish |
| 10 | **Publishing into a negative ranking state** | Wrong historical signal amplified; new content makes it worse | Audit → consolidate → fix links → then expand |
| 11 | **Judging results at 6–8 weeks** | Historical data lags ~6 months; premature pivots destroy momentum | Impressions at 90d, clicks at 180d; per-cluster analysis |
| 12 | **Zero information gain** | SERP-recombination content adds nothing; helpful-content guidance explicitly targets it | Every brief names its gain element (data, testing, expert, framework) |
| 13 | **Freezing the map** | Intents evolve; competitors expand borders; coverage is relative | Quarterly reconfiguration loop (Step 10) |
| 14 | **Ignoring the buyer's journey** | "Topical authority" coverage that never converts and doesn't serve the audience | Journey mapping in Step 2; funnel stage on every node |

# 12. Success Metrics

Measure per cluster, never just sitewide. Sequence expectations: **impressions → rankings → clicks → conversions**, in that order.

| Dimension | Metric | Tool | Target / Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Topical coverage** | Published nodes ÷ planned map nodes, per cluster | Map spreadsheet | ≥80% per active cluster before expanding borders |
| **Entity coverage** | Entities/attributes covered ÷ entity inventory; entities per 1,000 words on core pages | Entity map + NLP tools | Inventory ≥80% with attributes; 15–20 entities/1,000 words |
| **Organic visibility** | Impressions per cluster (leading indicator); share of voice for tracked topic queries | GSC (regex by URL path), rank tracker | Impressions rising within 60–90 days of cluster publish |
| **Internal link health** | Orphan count; avg click depth of quality nodes; % anchors matching target H1 | Screaming Frog / Sitebulb | 0 orphans; quality nodes ≤2 clicks; ≥80% descriptive anchors |
| **Rankings** | New / Lost / Shared queries per page; % of cluster queries in top 10 | GSC + rank tracker | New-query count growing per page = coverage being tested |
| **Traffic** | Organic sessions per cluster; assisted conversions from outer→core paths | GA4/analytics | Cluster-level growth at 3–6 months |
| **Engagement** | Engaged time, scroll, next-page rate on cluster pages; pogo-sticking proxies | GA4, Microsoft Clarity | Engagement is the raw material of historical data |
| **Search Console health** | Coverage/indexing status of map URLs; CTR vs. position per cluster | GSC | 100% of map URLs indexed; CTR at/above position benchmark |
| **AI visibility** (if relevant) | Citations/mentions of your pages in AI engines for topic prompts | AI-visibility tracking tools | Citation share growing with coverage — an increasingly used leading indicator, methodology still maturing **[DEBATED]** |

**Evaluation cadence:** 30d (indexing + impressions), 90d (impressions trend + new queries), 180d (rankings + clicks — first fair judgment of the map), 365d (authority state reassessment + border review).

# 13. Worked Example — B2B SaaS: An AI Visibility Tracking Platform

Monetization bridge: *teach how AI visibility works → prove the tool is the most rigorous way to measure it → convert through education-led trust.*
- **Central entity:** **AI visibility** (synonyms: AI search visibility, LLM visibility, generative search visibility).

# Executive Summary (one page)

**What this SOP does:** Turns topical authority from a buzzword into a repeatable production system: define who you are → choose the entity you'll own → map its complete query network from your commercial context → publish connected, consistent, evidence-rich coverage → measure the ranking-state shift per cluster.

**The theory in three lines:**
1. Topical authority is a **ranking state** earned when a site's coverage of an entity is complete, connected, and consistently engaged with over time (Topical Coverage × Historical Data).
2. Google's documented systems (site-level classifiers, E-E-A-T, semantic search) and patent literature (Topical TrustRank, categorical quality, phrase-based indexing, information gain) all converge on **topic-level, entity-based site evaluation** — no single "authority score" is documented, and this SOP never pretends one is.
3. Therefore the map — not the article — is the unit of strategy: **source context → central entity → central search intent → core section (monetize) + outer section (historical data) → nodes → bridges.**

**The process in ten steps:** (1) Source context & central entity → (2) Ontology, personas, journey → (3) Reverse-engineer one authority source → (4) Gather all query/entity data with provenance → (5) Cluster by SERP overlap; filter Relevant→Prominent→Popular → (6) Build the map with full metadata and borders → (7) Brief every node with contextual vectors and link plans → (8) Publish in cluster batches with factual consistency and momentum → (9) Evaluate per cluster: impressions → rankings → clicks, at 90/180 days → (10) Consolidate, reconfigure, then expand borders. If the site's ranking state is negative: stop publishing, audit and consolidate first.

**The five failure modes that kill most maps:** volume-first topic selection; no source context; page count mistaken for coverage; links as an afterthought; judging results before historical data can move (~6 months).
